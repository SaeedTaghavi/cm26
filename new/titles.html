Hadis Goodarzi

Magnetic behavior of flare stars on the Main-sequence and Sub-giant branch (Kepler Mission).
In addition to the existence of the planet, light curve of the stars provides useful information about their magnetic activity. We use the database of Kepler mission in order to extract and study the magnetic indicators such as starspots and flare events. Coverage and stability of the magnetic features are inspired by rotational modulation of the light-curve variations and flare activity of the stars are obtained using our automated flare detection algorithm. We analyze the light curve of 1740 flare stars from the mainsequence (MS) and 352 stars on the sub-giant branch (SGB) to study the relationship between the magnetic feature characteristic and the identified flare activity, then compare the results for these two different evolutionary states of the stars.

Mehrdad Mirbabayi

Uptunneling to de Sitter.
We propose a Euclidean preparation of an asymptotically AdS2 spacetime that contains an inflating dS2 bubble. The setup can be embedded in a four dimensional theory with a Minkowski vacuum and a false vacuum. AdS2 approximates the near horizon geometry of a two-sided near-extremal Reissner-Nordstr√∂m black hole, and the two sides can connect to the same Minkowski asymptotics to form a topologically nontrivial wormhole geometry. Likewise, in the false vacuum the near-horizon geometry of near-extremal black holes is approximately dS2 times 2-sphere. We interpret the Euclidean solution as describing the decay of an excitation inside the wormhole to a false vacuum bubble. The result is an inflating region inside a non-traversable asymptotically Minkowski wormhole. We speculate about the possibility of a brief period of causal connection when light matter fields are present.

Reza Rezaee

Recent advances in solar physics.
In this review talk, I present recent advances in the solar physics and in particular in our understanding of the interplay between a magnetic field and a gravitationally stratified plasma. From spectroscopic and spectropolarimetric observations of the solar spectrum in the infrared, visible, and the near-UV, we observe the tomography of the atmosphere in diverse spatial scales. The quest for a high spatial resolution led by advent of the adaptive optics about 20 years ago and space-borne measurements not only brought the solar physics to its golden age but presents challenges to our classical explanations at low resolution. While we are used to time-independent one-dimensional models, challenges posed by new observations hint that there is an ample amount of inhomogenity in the atmosphere. For instance this led to a revision of the old notion that structures in the solar atmosphere, e.g. in H-alpha, closely mimic the magnetic field lines. Numerical models elucidate the plasma dynamics in great details and assist observers to understand enigmatic features. The first three-dimensional simulation of a sunspot, emerged ten years ago, provide ideas about the flux rope twist and evolution from the convection zone where the field lines are anchored to the surface. The solar atmosphere is not just interesting because it is a plasma laboratory with a large Reynolds number. We study the magnetized plasma in high spatial, spectral, and temporal resolution that is not available on the ground.

Mahmood Roshan

Dynamical friction in spiral galaxies.
Dynamical friction plays a central role in the secular evolution of the spiral galaxies. To be specific, this friction reduces the pattern speed of the galactic bar and accordingly influences the angular momentum exchange throughout the disk. Since it is not possible to find an analytic description for dynamical friction, we use high-resolution N-body simulation to set up isolated disk galaxies. The evolution of the disk is explored in the absence and presence of the dark matter halo. Finally, we quantify the dynamical friction‚Äôs impact on the long-term evolution of the disk.

Elham Saremi

Monitoring survey of long period variable stars in the Local Group dwarf galaxies.
Presenting a complete suite of galactic environments, the Local Group (LG) dwarf galaxies are an excellent opportunity for studying the connection between stellar populations and galaxy evolution. In this regard, an optical monitoring survey of the majority of LG dwarf galaxies was carried out with the 2.5-m Isaac Newton Telescope (INT) to identify the most evolved asymptotic giant branch (AGB) stars and red supergiants that the most of them are Long Period Variable (LPV). These stars are at the end points of their evolution and therefore their luminosity can be directly translated into their birth mass; this enables us to reconstruct the star formation history. LPVs are also the main source of dust; in combination with Spitzer Space Telescope images at mid-IR wavelengths we will quantify the mass loss, and provide a detailed map of the mass feedback into the interstellar medium.

Soroush Shakeri

Towards the Detection of Axion Dark Matter.
The nature of dark matter (DM) particle is still unknown. One of the most interesting candidates for DM is axion or Axion Like Particles (ALPs) for many reasons. Research into the detectability of axion DM has recently revealed a vast numbers of promising experimental designs. Axions can couple to two photons and this property allows one to design high-precision optical setups to detect them. In fact, the Earth is traveling through an axion DM halo, so it is possible to capture axion induced electric signal by using a table-top experiment. Here, I am going to introduce a new way to probe ALPs based on Light by Light scattering process. Our scheme can potentially impact significantly on the discovery of ALPs as it covers a mostly overlooked and unexplored parameter region.

Alireza Vafaei Sadr

Mining cosmological datasets.
New generations of observations and simulations provide a huge amount of data, posing the big data problem, which makes scientists consider data science as an increasingly important player in almost all data-based projects. There are a lot of interesting ideas about applications of data science tools in astrophysics and cosmology. As a particular application, anomaly/outlier detection is one challenging area. An example of application is provided in astronomy by the LSST and SKA, the next-generation optical and radio telescopes which are expected to observe completely new types of celestial objects lurking in the torrent of data in the 100PB-10EB range. In this presentation, we will know about a general anomaly detection framework based on dimensionality reduction and unsupervised clustering (DRAMA). This approach identifies the primary prototypes in the data with anomalies detected by their large distances from the prototypes, either in the latent space or in the original, high-dimensional space.

Characterizing the relaxation state of galaxy systems.
Characterizing the relaxation state of galaxy systems is a necessary step for probing the influence of environment and halo dynamics on the evolution of galaxies. In this presentation, we will provide an assessment of the reliability of both the photometric and the spectroscopic probes of halo relaxation. We quantify the correlations between the halo relaxation or the dynamical age, which is defined based on the halo mass assembly in the cosmological simulations, with two spectroscopic indicators, namely the Anderson Darling statistic of the galaxy velocity distribution, A2, and the velocity-segregation, ‚àÜV. We will also investigate the correlation of the halo relaxation with photometric indicators, which are, primarily, the luminosity gap between the two most luminous galaxies within half the virial radius of a group, ‚àÜm12, complemented with the off-set between brightest group galaxies and the luminosity centroid, Do f f ‚àíset. We show that the combined ‚àÜm12 and Do f f‚àíset correlates more strongly with the dynamical age of the halo than the A2. Therefore we offer a reliable yet fast and economical method of quantifying the relaxation of galaxy systems.
